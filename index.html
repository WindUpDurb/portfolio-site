About

I am a full-stack Javascript developer and writer.


Hello, World

It was at “Hello, World” on Codecademy one Friday evening while working at a criminal defense law office in southern California when I ditched wavering desires to go to law school and accept wholeheartedly the pursuit of programming alongside writing fiction.


Iteration

After months of self-learning some Ruby and Javascript, I enrolled in a 14-week live-in full-stack Javascript coding boot-camp.

I am now working as an Instructional Software Developer at the coding boot-camp that I attended. My current main responsibilities consist of:


Building software, such as company products and instructional tools
  • I spearheaded a Chrome extension built on React and Redux that aimed at assisting the boot-camp’s graduates in their job searches
  • I spearheaded a Javascript Integrated Development Environment to be used for coding challenges for current students and for pre-work for prospective students
Creating and curating instructional content and resources
  • I organize and conduct whiteboard sessions with every student individually on a weekly basis
  • I conduct weekly one-on-one sessions with the students to review their code
Teaching and relating computer science and full-stack Javascript concepts to students
  • I am responsible for review sessions with each student for the weekly checklist of concepts
  • The live-in nature of the program allows me to be easily accessible to the students throughout the day and night to discuss, teach, and relate concepts, as well as debug and troubleshoot their code

In my pursuits, I have realized that writing stories and writing software is not all that different from one another.


There’s a piece of creative writing advice that is widely known in the literary community: “Kill your darlings.” It means that however attached an author may be to a word, sentence, paragraph, page, chapter, if it does not contribute to the piece as a whole, it should be taken out.

Applied to programming, this advice encourages taking into account micro and macro perspectives of a project, and how each line of code, function, component, feature, contributes to the project as a whole.


A story is not complete when the last period is placed. The real work is in revising draft after draft after draft to sharpen the language, remove the unnecessary, and bolster the essential. In programming, revising is synonymous with refractoring — the importance of debugging and optimizing code.
